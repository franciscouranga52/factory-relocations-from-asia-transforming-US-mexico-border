How factory relocations from Asia are transforming the U.S.-Mexico border

Employment and wages are soaring on both the Mexican and U.S. sides, making the border one of the most dynamic areas in North America

By Yi Liu and Francisco Uranga

Precast slab factory in Garcia, Nuevo Leon, Mexico / Grupo Compre (unsplash.com)

Five years ago, there were no Chinese restaurants in Salinas Victoria, Mexico. Things are different now in this town a few miles from Monterrey and close to the U.S. border. It is home to the Hofusan industrial park, where 27 Chinese factories have settled. They arrived as part of the wave of companies moving from Asia to North America to be closer to the U.S. market. Next year it will add Sunon, a Chinese furniture company.

"The trade war between the U.S. and China accelerated our decision," said YinJiao Shi, the public representative of Sunon about building in Mexico a factory that will hire between 400 and 500 workers when it becomes operational. "The main thing is that it is close to the U.S. market and also has tariff advantages."

Sunon is one of the Chinese companies that decided to move to Mexico since 2018. The first boost came from tariffs imposed by Donald Trump that year and maintained by the Biden administration. A further jump occurred with the pandemic when nearly half of small companies had difficulty keeping up with customer demand in deliveries due to supply chain problems, according to the U.S. Chamber of Commerce. This led many to consider moving closer to the U.S. market. On the one hand to reduce geopolitical risk and on the other to minimize the financial cost of holding inventory on ships. The consequence of this shift is the transformation of a specific strip of land: the U.S.-Mexico border.

Between 2016 and 2021, employment in Mexico's border cities increased by 54%, according to the National Occupation and Employment Survey (ENOE, for its acronym in Spanish) data. In the rest of the country, it grew by only 11% in the same period. This shows strong dynamics in this area. During these years, the border population grew by 15%.

While one of the drivers of this change is the arrival of factories from Asia, a process known as nearshoring, the effect is not limited to manufacturing. It also extends to complementary services, such as Chinese restaurants in Salinas Victoria.

"Society has changed at the border. When you grow, you have more spending power and generate more economic spillover. More hotels and restaurants are built," said Luis Manuel Hernández González, president of INDEX, the National Council of the Maquiladora and Manufacturing Export Industry. "Companies are not looking to replace large supply chains from China, but rather small substitution pieces."

The spillover effect in the area also extends to local suppliers such as the firm of engineer Elliot Pineda, who runs a software development company in Hermosillo, Sonora. Most of his clients are manufacturing companies on both sides of the border. Pineda found it was an opportunity to grow because it allowed him to earn 30% more if he sold services to American companies.

"Wages for software developers are improving. So much so that they do not necessarily want to move to the U.S. They think that with what they earn they can have a better quality of life in Mexico because the cost of living is cheaper," Pineda said. "Not only do engineers get paid higher, but they have interesting jobs with a more Silicon Valley culture."

The average hourly manufacturing wage in the U.S. is almost eight times higher than in Mexico, according to INEGI data. Despite the gap, since 2016 that difference has narrowed.

Employment in the Mexican export manufacturing sector increased by 13% at the border between 2016 and 2021, about the same as in the rest of the country. But manufacturing wages rose 28% at the border states, above inflation, while 17% in the rest of the country. In previous years, wages had grown at a similar pace, with a slight advantage in favor of manufacturing wages in non-border states.

Wage dynamics at the border are explained by two reasons, according to Hernández González. The first is the type of manufacturing, including medical in Tijuana, aerospace in Sonora, and automobile in Juárez. These sectors and their close ties to the U.S. drive wages upward. The second reason is the increase in the minimum wage decreed in 2018 by Mexican President Andres Manuel Lopez Obrador to retain workers at the border.

In fact, Sunon faced difficulties retaining staff.

"In Mexico, hiring workers is not as good as in China. The culture is particularly different," said Sunon's public representative. "Now we are building a factory. We pay workers a weekly wage. If they are not satisfied, they will not return the following week. So we try to offer better benefits and higher wages to our workers to retain them."

Despite the pandemic's momentum, the process of leaving Asia and seeking closer markets is a long-term trend. It began at least in 2012, according to Harry Moser, founder of the Reshoring Institute.

The main reasons include the convergence of production costs between the U.S. and China, Moser said. This is due to the increase in American productivity and the rise in Chinese labor costs. That adds to the inconvenience for a U.S. company of having production so far away. Moser explains that reshoring —moving factories to the U.S.— and nearshoring are part of the same process of bringing production closer together. Moser's data shows that since he began tracking announcements, some 1.6 million manufacturing jobs related to reshoring have been opened in the U.S, more than half of them in the South region. And while pre-2000 employment levels have not recovered, the decline is reversing.

According to the latest Reshoring Initiative data report, between 2010 and 2021, nearshoring also created more than 40,000 jobs in Mexico.

The U.S. continues to lose jobs to Mexico because of lower labor costs and proximity, Moser admitted. "But the trend is for Mexican wages to increase and that balances things out," Moser said. "They are happy and we are happy."

The U.S. side data are less clear on manufacturing jobs growth, but show the boom at the border in sectors such as transportation and construction.

One of the winners of the buoyant border economy is David Delgado, a 25-year construction veteran based in Laredo, Texas. Although he noted that the area's economy has been strong since NAFTA in 1994, the last few years have seen acceleration. More and more trucks cross the border and more warehouses are built, he said. Because of the high demand for work, he quit his construction job and started working as a freelance supervisor for building projects. His annual income grew from $38,000 to $110,000, thanks to his bilingual advantage in an industry where most workers do not speak English.

If there are forces driving factory relocation from China, there are also others holding it back.

Rosemary Coats, Executive Director of the Reshoring Institute, warns that although China no longer offers cheap labor, not all companies are willing to leave the country. Coats pointed out that this could mean losing presence in a growing market, something that could run against the firm's strategy.

Other reasons to slow down the process are in the country of destination. Mexico has certain restrictions that could keep nearshoring investments from taking off, warns Ana Gutiérrez, Foreign Trade and Labor Market Coordinator at the Mexican Institute for Competitiveness (IMCO, for its acronym in Spanish). Gutiérrez pointed to infrastructure deficits in roads, electricity or water services, and a lack of skilled workers. This is critical because some of the industries relocating have sophisticated components, such as semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, or electric cars.

Sunon appreciates the efforts of the Nuevo León government in expanding the highways leading to the U.S. border to facilitate transportation from their factories. Yet, they express major concerns over raw material sourcing, as finding high-quality, affordable wood in Mexico similar to what they could procure in China proves challenging.

"Mexico's supply chain is far from perfect," said Yinjiao Shi. "In the long run, this is our most significant concern."
